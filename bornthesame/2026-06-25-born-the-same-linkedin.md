# LinkedIn Post

No one is born knowing how to walk, talk, lead, sell, or connect.

We forget this constantly. We watch someone who seems brilliant in their twenties and assume they were handed something the rest of us missed. But look closer at almost any "natural," and you find the same thing: early access to the right knowledge, years of quiet practice, and enough repetition for the skill to become part of them.

I learned this the hard way. I was the shyest kid in my high school — three friends, no idea how to start a conversation, convinced something was wrong with me. It took about three years of deliberately learning and practicing, but I stopped being socially awkward. The skill didn't arrive. I built it.

That's the part the "natural talent" story hides. What we call genius is usually the visible result of an invisible process: get the right knowledge, internalize it until it's no longer something you remember but something you do, then practice it until it feels automatic.

The most useful consequence of this is freedom. If ability is built, then being a late starter is not a disqualification. Age changes the context, not the possibility. A forty-year-old with focus and fewer distractions can reach mastery a child took twenty years to stumble into.

We are born the same. What we become is a decision, not an inheritance.

What's one skill you've been treating as fixed that might just be unbuilt?
